Module 1
How Startups Think About Growth

📈 Growth rate > current size — always

In your investment world, you looked at a company's current AUM or revenue. In startups, what matters far more is the rate of growth — specifically, week-over-week or month-over-month percentage growth.

💡 Finance analogy
When you evaluated a fund manager, you looked at alpha — returns above the benchmark. For startups, growth rate IS the alpha. A company at $1M ARR growing 20% monthly will overtake a company at $10M ARR growing 5% monthly within 12 months.

Paul Graham (Y Combinator) defines a startup as "a company designed to grow fast." The target? 5–7% week-over-week for early-stage. That sounds small — but compounded, it's 10–13x in a year.

Weekly growth → 12x/year

Weekly growth → 142x/year

Weekly growth → 2.8x/year

🧩 Product vs. Marketing vs. Growth — what's the difference?

These three words get thrown around interchangeably. Here's how to think about them:

Product — What you build. Features, UX, the core experience. Makes the thing people want to use.
Marketing — How you create awareness and desire. Brand, content, ads, PR. Makes people want to try it.
Growth — The bridge. Uses data + experiments to accelerate the loop between Product and Marketing. Gets more people to the "aha moment" faster.

At a tiny startup, one person does all three. At a Series B company, these are separate teams. GTM (Go-to-Market) is the plan for how you bring a product to market — it sits across all three.

🎯 Your target role
Growth/Marketing/GTM at an AI startup means you'll touch all of this — running experiments, writing copy, analyzing funnels, launching campaigns, and talking to users. It's the most cross-functional role in a startup.

🔁 The Growth Loop (not a funnel)

Old-school marketing thought in funnels — pour people in the top, some convert at the bottom. Modern growth thinks in loops — each user action creates the conditions for the next user.

Acquire user → Deliver value → User shares/refers → New user acquired → 🔄

When a loop works, growth is compounding — like compound interest. This is why investors love network effects. Every VC you've met who said "we look for defensibility" — this is a big part of what they meant.

💡 Finance analogy
A growth loop is like a reinvestment rate. A fund that reinvests 100% of gains compounds faster than one that distributes them. Viral loops = 100% reinvestment rate on user acquisition.
Case study
🤖
ChatGPT — 0 to 100M users in 60 days
AI Case Study ▼

The setup

OpenAI launched ChatGPT on November 30, 2022 — with zero marketing budget and almost no pre-launch hype. It wasn't even their primary product (that was the API).

Within 5 days: 1 million users. Within 2 months: 100 million. Fastest consumer product to 100M in history at the time.

User growth timeline

1M
Day 5
10M
Day 40
100M
Day 60
180M
~6 mos

Why did it grow so fast? (no ad budget!)

  • Inherent shareability — outputs are designed to be screenshot and shared. "Look what AI did" is social currency.
  • Word of mouth loops — every user who shared a ChatGPT screenshot on Twitter/LinkedIn acquired ~3-5 new users.
  • Timing — the market was primed. People had heard about AI for years. This was the first time it felt real and accessible.
  • Free tier — zero friction to try. No credit card, no sales process. Classic PLG (Product-Led Growth).
  • Media amplification — tech journalists had something genuinely new to write about, which drove organic traffic.

The growth loop in action

User generates surprising output → Screenshots it → Posts on social → Followers try it → They generate outputs → They share too → 🔄

What a growth PM would have focused on

  • Making the "wow moment" happen in the first 60 seconds (activation)
  • Adding a "share this conversation" button (virality)
  • Monitoring which use cases drove the most word of mouth
  • Keeping the free tier generous enough to spread, while converting power users to Plus ($20/mo)
💡 Key takeaway
ChatGPT didn't grow because of marketing. It grew because the product itself created shareable moments. This is the holy grail of growth — when your product IS your marketing engine.
