Module 1
How Startups Think About Growth

📈 Growth rate > current size — always

In your investment world, you looked at AUM or revenue. In startups, what matters far more is rate of growth — week-over-week or month-over-month percentage.

💡 Finance analogy

Growth rate IS the alpha. A company at $1M ARR growing 20%/month will overtake a company at $10M ARR growing 5%/month in ~12 months. Current size is just the starting point.

Paul Graham's definition: "A startup is a company designed to grow fast." Target: 5–7% week-over-week in early stage = 10–13x per year compounded.

5%
Weekly → 12x/year
10%
Weekly → 142x/year
2%
Weekly → 2.8x/year

🧩 Product vs. Marketing vs. Growth vs. GTM

Product — What you build. Makes the thing people want to use.
Marketing — Creates awareness and desire. Makes people want to try it.
Growth — The bridge. Uses data + experiments to get more people to the "aha moment" faster.

GTM (Go-to-Market) = the plan for bringing a product to its target market. Spans all three.

🎯 Your target role

Growth/GTM at an AI startup = running experiments, writing copy, analyzing funnels, launching campaigns, talking to users. Most cross-functional role at a startup.

🔁 Growth Loops (not funnels)

Old-school: funnels — pour in users, some convert at the bottom. Modern growth: loops — each user action creates the next user.

Acquire → Deliver value → User shares → New user → 🔄
💡 Finance analogy

A loop is like a 100% reinvestment rate. Every VC who said "we look for defensibility" — growth loops are part of what they meant. Compounding user acquisition beats linear funnels every time.

Module 2
The Metrics That Matter

🪣 AARRR — The Pirate Metrics

Dave McClure's AARRR framework is the standard funnel for startup metrics. Every growth role uses this.

A — Acquisition: How do users find you? (SEO, ads, word of mouth, referral)
A — Activation: Do users have a good first experience? Did they reach the "aha moment"?
R — Retention: Do users come back? This is the most important "R".
R — Revenue: Do users pay? (MRR, ARR, LTV, ARPU)
R — Referral: Do users refer others? (viral coefficient, NPS)
💡 Finance analogy

AARRR = a fund's investment lifecycle. Acquisition = deal sourcing. Activation = due diligence & close. Retention = LP relationship. Revenue = returns. Referral = getting introduced to new LPs by existing ones.

⭐ The North Star Metric

Every great growth team focuses on ONE metric that best captures the value delivered to users. Everything else is a lever to move it.

Airbnb
Nights booked
Spotify
Time listening
Slack
Messages sent
Duolingo
Daily active users (streaks)
Notion
Blocks created
ChatGPT
Conversations per user
⚠️ Common mistake

Vanity metrics like total signups or app downloads feel good but don't predict success. A North Star must capture real value delivered — not just traffic.

🔒 Retention is Everything

You can acquire millions of users and still fail. If they don't come back, you have a leaky bucket. Retention = product-market fit signal.

The retention curve: Plot % of users who return each week/month. If it flattens above 0%, you have retention. If it hits 0%, you don't.

💡 Finance analogy

Retention = portfolio duration. LTV (Lifetime Value) is the DCF of a customer relationship. High retention = long duration asset. Low retention = you're constantly paying to refill a leaky bucket — like a fund with no LP lock-up that has constant redemptions.

5%
Improve retention → 25–95% more profit (Bain study)
DAU/MAU
Ratio = engagement health (40%+ = excellent)
Case study
🦉
Duolingo — How streaks became a North Star (and saved the company)
Retention Case Study ▼

The problem

Duolingo had millions of downloads but terrible retention. Users signed up, did one lesson, and never came back. Downloads was a vanity metric masking a broken product.

The insight

Their growth team discovered that users who built a streak of 3+ days had dramatically higher long-term retention. The streak became emotionally motivating — people didn't want to break it.

What they did

  • Made streaks the centerpiece of the app UI — visible, celebrated
  • Added "streak freeze" (streak protection when you miss a day) — reduces anxiety without breaking behavior
  • Push notifications: "Your streak is in danger! 🔥" — highly effective re-engagement
  • Weekly leaderboards tied to streak performance

Results

DAU grew from ~5M to 40M+ over 5 years. Duolingo went public in 2021 at a $5.7B valuation. Retention-driven growth.

💡 Key takeaway

The right North Star metric (DAU driven by streaks, not just downloads) changed everything. Measure what creates real value, not what looks good in a deck.

Product-Led Growth (PLG)

🏃 PLG vs Sales-Led Growth (SLG)

Two fundamentally different ways to grow. Most AI startups today choose PLG — and for good reason.

PLG (Product-Led): The product sells itself. Free tier → users find value → upgrade → invite teammates. Low CAC, scales infinitely.
SLG (Sales-Led): Salespeople sell the product. SDRs, demos, contracts, procurement. High CAC, doesn't scale, but works for enterprise.
💡 Finance analogy

PLG = passive index investing — low cost, compounding returns, scales automatically. SLG = active management — high-touch, expensive, but sometimes necessary for large allocations (enterprise deals). Most early startups should start PLG, like most investors should start passive.

$1.18
CAC of PLG vs $1.14 for SLG (but PLG scales)
58%
of SaaS companies now use PLG (OpenView 2023)

⚡ The "Aha Moment" — most critical concept in PLG

The "aha moment" is the specific instant a user first experiences your product's core value. Everything in PLG is optimized to get users there as fast as possible.

Slack: First time a team sends 2,000 messages together
Dropbox: First time a file syncs across two devices
Figma: First time you see someone else's cursor in your file
ChatGPT: First output that makes you say "wait, it actually understands me"
🎯 Growth PM's job

Map the user journey. Find the aha moment. Remove every step between sign-up and that moment. Shorten time-to-wow from days → minutes → seconds.

🔧 PLG Tactics for AI Products

Freemium: Give enough value free to hook users, charge for power features. (ChatGPT free → $20/mo Plus)
Viral sharing: Build share buttons into outputs. Loom videos, Figma links, Midjourney images all carry the brand when shared.
Usage-based pricing: Charge per API call, per image, per seat. Low barrier to start, scales with value.
In-product upgrade nudges: Show the premium feature, blur it, let them touch it. "You've used 80% of your free credits."
Case study
🎨
Figma — How PLG ate Adobe's lunch
PLG Case Study ▼

The situation (2012)

Adobe had a near-monopoly on design tools. Photoshop and Illustrator required expensive licenses, desktop installs, and were impenetrable to non-designers. Sharing files was a nightmare.

Figma's PLG strategy

  • Free forever tier — designers could use Figma with no credit card, no install, no IT approval
  • Real-time collaboration — the aha moment was seeing another cursor move in your file. It spread virally within design teams
  • Shareable links — every Figma file could be shared via URL. Developers, PMs, and clients got exposure to Figma just by clicking a link
  • Bottom-up expansion — one designer adopts Figma → shares with team → team adopts → company buys enterprise plan

The result

Adobe acquired Figma in 2022 for $20B — the largest design software acquisition ever. The deal was blocked by regulators, but the valuation was real. PLG won.

💡 Key takeaway

Figma's product was designed to spread. Every shared file was a demo. Every new collaborator was a potential customer. The product did the selling.

Module 4
Go-to-Market (GTM)

🎯 ICP — Ideal Customer Profile

The #1 GTM mistake: trying to sell to everyone. The most successful AI startups start with a brutally specific ICP and expand from there.

Bad ICP: "SMBs who need productivity tools"
Good ICP: "Early-stage B2B SaaS founders, 5-30 employees, using Notion + Linear, frustrated that their CRM doesn't integrate with their dev tools, spending 4+ hours/week on manual status updates"

The more specific, the better your messaging, the better your channels, the better your conversion.

💡 Finance analogy

ICP = target asset class. A good allocator doesn't "invest in everything." They pick a specific category (private credit, growth equity, VC) and go deep. Trying to serve every customer = a fund with no investment thesis = mediocre returns everywhere.

Key ICP dimensions: Job title, company size, industry, tech stack, pain point, budget authority, buying trigger

🗣️ Positioning

Positioning is how you carve out a unique place in the customer's mind relative to alternatives. Not your features — your story.

The positioning formula: "For [ICP], [Product] is the only [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe]."

Example — Perplexity AI: "For researchers and knowledge workers, Perplexity is the only AI search engine that gives you cited, real-time answers instead of making you verify sources yourself."

⚡ Category design vs category entry

Category entry: "We're like [existing product] but better." (Safer but crowded)
Category design: "We invented a new category." (Risky but creates a monopoly — think Salesforce inventing "CRM cloud")

📡 Channel Strategy

Channels = how you reach your ICP. Not all channels are equal. Match your channel to where your ICP actually spends time.

Owned channels: Content/blog, email list, community, app. You control them, they compound. Slow to build, zero marginal cost.
Earned channels: Press/PR, word of mouth, SEO rankings, influencer mentions. Hard to get, but hugely credible.
Paid channels: Google/Meta/LinkedIn ads, sponsorships, influencer paid deals. Fast to start, stops when you stop paying. High CAC.
💡 Finance analogy

Owned = bonds (steady, reliable income). Earned = real estate (appreciates over time, creates passive returns). Paid = active trading (works while you're at the desk, stops when you log off). A good GTM strategy diversifies across all three.

Case study
⚡
Linear — Positioning against Jira with developer-first GTM
GTM Case Study ▼

The market in 2019

Jira dominated project management for engineering teams. It was powerful but notoriously slow, complex, and disliked by developers. Atlassian had a sales-first, top-down GTM.

Linear's ICP and positioning

Linear targeted a hyper-specific ICP: senior engineers and CTOs at fast-growing tech startups who valued speed and design. Their positioning: "Project management built for the speed of thought."

Their GTM playbook

  • Developer-first: Released keyboard shortcuts, CLI tools, GitHub integration on day one. Won over power users who convinced their teams.
  • Design as signal: Made the most beautiful project management tool ever built. Design-conscious engineers shared it on Twitter constantly.
  • Invite-only waitlist: Created exclusivity and FOMO. Engineers wanted to be early. Twitter buzz drove the waitlist to 50K.
  • Bottom-up PLG: Free tier for small teams. One engineer adopts → convinces team → company pays.

Result

Linear reached a $400M+ valuation with a tiny team and no traditional sales motion. Word of mouth from developers was their primary channel.

💡 Key takeaway

Razor-sharp ICP + positioning that resonated with a passionate niche (senior devs who hated Jira) + channels those users actually use (Twitter, GitHub) = GTM that worked without a sales team.

Content, SEO & Organic Flywheels

📈 Content as a Compounding Asset

Paid ads stop the moment you stop paying. Content keeps working for years. It's the closest thing to a passive income stream in marketing.

80%
HubSpot's traffic comes from posts 2+ years old
6-12 mo
Typical time for a blog post to rank on Google
💡 Finance analogy

Paid ads = renting office space (monthly cost, gone when lease ends). Content = buying the building (upfront investment, but you own an asset that appreciates). Every article you publish is a new asset that generates returns over time. Content compounds like a long-duration bond.

🎯 AI company content opportunity

Most AI companies are too focused on product to build content. The ones that do — Midjourney's tutorials, Anthropic's research blog, Perplexity's use case guides — own massive organic channels competitors can't easily replicate.

🔍 SEO for AI Products — the Practical Version

SEO = getting Google to send you free users by ranking high for search terms your ICP types.

Use case pages: "AI for [specific job to be done]" — e.g. "AI for legal contract review", "AI for sales email writing". High intent, low competition.
Comparison pages: "[Your product] vs [competitor]" — users in buying mode search these. High conversion.
Glossary & definition pages: "What is [AI term]?" — captures top-of-funnel, brand awareness. Drives newsletter signups.
Integration pages: "[Your product] + [popular tool]" — e.g. "Notion AI for Slack teams". Captures users of adjacent tools.

Key insight: search intent > keyword volume. A high-intent term with 100 searches/month beats a vanity term with 100K searches where no one is buying.

🔁 The Content Flywheel

A flywheel builds momentum over time. Each rotation is faster than the last.

Publish content → Rank on Google → Traffic grows → Leads → customers → Customer stories & case studies → More content to publish → 🔄

HubSpot built a $20B company almost entirely on this flywheel. They published 500+ blog posts/month at peak. Their blog now gets 7M+ visits/month with no paid acquisition.

Case study
📝
Notion — How templates became an SEO army
Content SEO Case Study ▼

The insight

Notion realized their users were building templates — meeting agendas, project trackers, OKR frameworks, reading lists. Each template was a potential SEO asset targeting a specific search query.

What they built

  • Template gallery: 10,000+ templates, each optimized for a specific use case. "Notion project management template", "Notion CRM template", "Notion weekly review template" — each ranking for its own keyword.
  • User-generated content at scale: Community members created templates. Notion just needed to curate and publish them. Zero marginal cost.
  • SEO moat: 50,000+ pages indexed by Google, each targeting a long-tail keyword. Competitors can't easily replicate years of accumulated SEO.

The result

Notion gets millions of organic visits per month from template searches alone. Users searching for "productivity template" find Notion, create an account, and get hooked — all without a paid ad.

💡 Key takeaway

Module 6
Community, Virality & Network Effects

🕸️ Network Effects — the Strongest Moat in Tech

A network effect exists when a product becomes more valuable as more people use it. This is the most powerful defensive moat in technology.

Direct network effects: More users = more value for all users. (WhatsApp, Slack — worthless alone, invaluable with your team)
Indirect network effects: More users on one side benefits the other side. (App Store: more users → more developers → more apps → more users)
Data network effects: More users generate more data → better AI model → better product → more users. Unique to AI companies.
💡 Finance analogy

Network effects = an economic moat. Warren Buffett's moat analogy — businesses with network effects are like a city: the more businesses that set up there, the more valuable the location becomes, making it increasingly expensive for competitors to set up a rival city. This is why VCs pay 100x revenue for network-effect businesses.

🦠 Virality — the K-Factor

The K-factor measures how viral your product is. K = (invitations sent per user) × (% who convert from those invitations).

Example: Each user invites 5 people. 20% convert. K = 5 × 0.20 = 1.0
K < 1
Growing but needs other channels
K > 1
Viral — grows exponentially on its own

Most products have K < 1. That's OK — even K = 0.3 meaningfully reduces your CAC. Design for virality: add share buttons, make outputs public by default, build collaboration features.

💬 Community-Led Growth

A community is owned audience. Unlike followers on Twitter or LinkedIn (rented from a platform), your Discord/Slack/email list is an asset you control.

Why community works for AI products: Users want to share prompts, tips, and outputs. There's a natural community around "getting better at using this tool."
Community flywheel: Build community → members share content → more people discover you → they join the community → share more content → 🔄
💡 Owned vs rented audience

Twitter/Instagram followers = renting. Algorithm changes wipe you out overnight (ask any creator from 2021 who relied on Instagram reach). Discord/email list = owning. You can reach 100% of your audience anytime.

Results

💡 Key takeaway

Making the product social by default (public generations in Discord) created a community AND a viral engine AND a support channel simultaneously. The product experience WAS the community.

AI-Specific Growth Patterns

🎬 The Demo as a Growth Channel

AI products are uniquely demoable. A 60-second video showing something impossible (or magical) drives more signups than any ad campaign.

This is a new growth channel that didn't exist before AI. The demo video IS the acquisition funnel for many AI products.

v0.dev (Vercel): One tweet showing "type a UI in plain English → get working React code" → 40K waitlist in 48 hours
Cursor: Demo showing AI writing a full feature from a comment → viral across developer Twitter
Kling AI: One video of a cat eating watermelon (absurdly realistic) → millions of views, massive signups
💡 Finance analogy

A demo is like a fund's pitch at a LP day — you have 3 minutes to make them feel the potential. Unlike a deck full of numbers, a live demo makes people feel "I need this." The best demos don't explain — they show something that makes jaws drop.

⚡ Engineering the "Wow Moment"

The wow moment is the instant a user experiences something that feels magical or impossible. AI products are uniquely positioned to create these moments. Growth teams obsess over them.

Time-to-wow matters enormously: If users don't hit the wow moment in their first session, they may never return. Reduce steps to zero. Remove signup friction. Show value before you ask for an email.

Framework: FTUE (First Time User Experience) = the sequence from landing page → signup → first action → WOW. Every step that adds friction kills conversion.

of users who don't activate in first session never return
<60s
Target: wow moment within 60 seconds of first use

🔒 AI-Specific Growth Moats

AI companies can build moats that are structurally harder for competitors to replicate than traditional software.

Data flywheel: Usage → training data → better model → more usage. The longer you run, the harder to catch up. (Why OpenAI's head start matters)
Workflow lock-in: Once a team integrates your AI into their daily workflow, switching costs are high. (Cursor users can't easily go back to VS Code)
Personalization compounds: AI that learns your preferences, writing style, or codebase becomes more valuable over time. Your data is in there.
🎯 Growth implication

For AI products, getting users deeply integrated into their workflow early (even at a loss) is more valuable than short-term monetization. The switching cost becomes your moat. This is why Cursor, GitHub Copilot, and ChatGPT all have aggressive free tiers.
